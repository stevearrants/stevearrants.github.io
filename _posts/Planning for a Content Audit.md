# Planning for a Content Audit
One of my recent jobs was to convert existing documentation to the parent company's brand and format.
I suggested also doing a content audit as one hadn't been done before. The documentation was the
specifications "prettied up." The manager of the documentation group was pretty insistent that we 
didn't need to do a content audit. The Validation group would catch anything. But what about writing
docs for the end user? That, I was told, wasn't our job.

>Let that be a warning! Any time some manager says writing for the end user isn't
in your job description, you not writing. You're merely *typing*.

So what *is* a Content Audit? A content audit is a process to collect, analyze, and report on your
user manuals, online help, and other documents against specific performance criteria.
Conducting a content audit of your technical documentation is essential for ensuring clarity, accuracy, and usefulness. Here's a list of things to help get you started:

1. **Define Your Goals**: What do you want to achieve with your content audit? Are you looking to improve readability, update outdated information, ensure consistency, or identify content gaps? You can do one, some, or all of these. Of course, the more tasks, the longer it can take.

2. **Inventory Your Content**: Gather all your technical documentation. Create a comprehensive list, including titles, authors, publication dates, and locations (URLs or file paths). Create a spreadsheet or Notion document with this information. Make it shareable among the team so that it can be quickly updated.

3. **Develop Evaluation Criteria**: Establish a set of criteria for evaluating your content. Common criteria include accuracy, relevance, clarity, consistency, completeness, and alignment with user needs.

4. **Assess Your Content**: Review each piece of content against your evaluation criteria. Take notes on areas that need improvement, such as outdated information, unclear instructions, or inconsistencies.

5. **Gather Feedback**: Collect feedback from users, stakeholders, and team members. This can provide valuable insights into how the documentation is being used and what improvements are needed.

6. **Analyze and Prioritize**: Analyze your findings and prioritize the issues based on their impact and urgency. Create a plan for addressing the most critical issues first.

7. **Update and Improve**: Make the necessary updates to your documentation. This may involve rewriting sections, adding new content, updating links, or standardizing terminology.

8. **Create a Maintenance Plan**: Establish a regular schedule for reviewing and updating your documentation. This will help ensure that your content remains accurate and useful over time.

9. **Monitor and Measure**: Track the effectiveness of your updates by monitoring user feedback and engagement. Use this data to continuously improve your documentation.

Performing a thorough content audit can be time-consuming, but it’s worth the effort to ensure that your technical documentation meets the needs of your users. If you need more detailed guidance on any of these steps, just let me know!
